Full Article

Paragraph 1
Gen-Z is changing dating rules around the world. They want deep connections but also want to protect their mental health. Many young people now use "6-7 dating," where they only talk or text for one hour a day. This helps them stay balanced with work and school. Experts say this style of dating is "manageable, low-pressure, and realistic" for busy young people. They also use "ick lists" to track things they do not like in a partner, such as being rude or having no hobbies.

Paragraph 2
In Japan, dating is becoming more relaxed. Many young Japanese people have an "if it happens, it happens" attitude. Instead of dating, some prefer "oshikatsu," which is spending time and money to support famous idols or actors. Professor Masahiro Yamada says some students even break up with partners to focus more on their hobbies. Because of this, Gen-Z in Japan is less desperate to find a partner than their parents were.

Paragraph 3
Dating apps are now a normal way to meet in Japan. About 25% of young married couples in Japan met through an app. Japanese apps focus on hobbies and "honne," which means honest feelings. For example, the app Pairs has "My Tags" for very specific interests like certain games or even "the smell of osmanthus trees". This helps people with niche hobbies find a good match more easily.

Paragraph 4
However, many Gen-Z daters feel nervous about starting deep conversations. Around 84% of daters want deeper connections, but they often wait for the other person to speak first. They prefer "micro-commitments," like adding someone to a "Close Friends" list on social media, before making big promises. Even with these challenges, 80% of Gen-Z still believe they will find true love in the future.
